Prescription opioids: The importance of developing formulations with abuse-deterrent properties

Prescription opioids have long played an important role in pain management. However, abuse and misuse of these products have created a serious and growing public health issue.1 Prescription opioid analgesic tablets can be manipulated for abuse by being crushed and swallowed, crushed and snorted, smoked, or the crushed tablets are dissolved in a solution and injected.1 In an effort to address the problem of misuse and abuse and ensure that patients in pain can continue to have access to opioid analgesics, in 2013 FDA developed draft guidance to help assist pharmaceutical companies seeking to develop opioids with abuse-deterrent properties.1

Helping deter abuse and misuse

FDA cites the development of opioid analgesics with abuse-deterrent properties as one potentially important step in helping to deter abuse and misuse.1 Opioid formulations with abuse-deterrent properties are designed to target specific routes of abuse and can be formulated in a number of different ways, such as creating physical/chemical barriers to manipulation, adding an opioid antagonist, or combining with substances that produce unpleasant effects and interfere with the euphoric effects associated with abuse.1 The methods used in the development of opioids with abuse-deterrent properties often focus on making the product more difficult to manipulate, or on rendering the manipulated product less gratifying to the abuser.1

Opioids with abuse-deterrent properties

The development of opioids with abuse-deterrent properties is relatively recent, involving new technologies as well as new methods for evaluating the effectiveness of these agents.1 FDA considers the development of opioids with abuse-deterrent properties a public health priority and has created draft guidance regarding the types of clinical studies that should be conducted, how these studies should be evaluated, and what labeling claims are most appropriate for these agents.1 Though no product with abuse-deterrent properties is un-abuseable, the development of opioid formulations with these characteristics is nonetheless an important step in addressing the issue of opioid abuse and misuse.1
