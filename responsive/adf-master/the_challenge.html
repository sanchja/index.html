Addressing the challenge1

Prescription opioids are subject to abuse

Abuse of prescription analgesics is on the rise. Between 2004 and 2012, the number of people who were dependent upon or who abused prescription analgesics in the US increased from 1.4 million to 2.1 million.

Methods of abuse and misuse of prescription opioids

There are a number of methods by which prescription opioids can be manipulated, including1,3:

  • Crushing pills for snorting or smoking
  • Dissolving pills in liquid for injecting
  • Taking a number of pills in excess of the prescribed dosage
  • Chewing pills
  • Coadministering with alcohol or other substances

Common Tampering Methods and Routes of Abuse1,3

Individuals who alter the prescribed opioid formulation are looking to change the rate of release or amount of the drug released into their systems so they can achieve a rapid “high.”1,3 Manipulating the pill by tampering with its physical makeup can create a “dose-dump” effect, resulting in a higher peak serum concentration (Cmax) of the drug over a shorter period of time (Tmax).3 Extended-release (ER) formulations are more attractive to abusers than immediate-release (IR) formulations, because of the greater amount of drug contained within the product. Tampering with extended-release products can result in an onset of action that is more rapid than that intended by the manufacturer, thereby inducing a greater sense of euphoria in the abuser.3

Diversion

Although patients prescribed opioids for legitimate pain needs have been known to abuse them, very often opioids are diverted from the intended recipient for abuse by others.2 However, despite the inherently illegal nature of such diversion, criminals or drug dealers are not the most common source.2 The2012 National Survey on Drug Use and Health reported that nearly 70% of people who abuse prescription analgesics for nonmedical use get them from friends or family members—either by stealing or buying them, or being given them for free.2

Growing concern over prescribing opioids

The increase in abuse and misuse of opioids has raised concerns and sparked a debate among healthcare professionals.3 With the mounting social concerns over opioid misuse, abuse, and diversion, 2013 FDA guidance encourages pharmaceutical companies to develop opioid formulations with abuse-deterrent properties.1 Although prescription opioids can be subject to abuse, concerns about abuse, misuse, and diversion should not prevent the proper management of patients in pain.1
